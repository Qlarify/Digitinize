Frameworks / Corporate Storytelling

A narrative that travels — origin to resolution.

Most corporate "storytelling" doesn't survive past the first scene. The vocabulary is borrowed, the structure is missing, and the audience can feel the difference inside fifteen seconds. Real story has architecture — origin, tension, decision, stakes, resolution. We use a 5-arc framework on every brand film, founder series, and content system we build. Here it is, in plain form, with the diagnostic that turns a business into a story.

Why story

Why most corporate stories don't move anyone.

Three patterns show up across stalled brand films. Each is a structural failure — the brand had something to say; the architecture was missing.

📋

Mission read aloud

The film recites the mission statement. The viewer hears a value proposition. There is no protagonist, no friction, no decision being made — just a polished claim with B-roll.

⚙️

Features dressed as narrative

The "story" is a product feature list with emotive lighting. The vocabulary borrows from film; the architecture borrows from a brochure.

😶

No stakes

Nothing is at risk in the film. Nothing was at risk in the founding. The audience cannot care, because the brand isn't risking anything by telling this version of itself.

🤝

Wrong protagonist

The company casts itself as the hero. The strongest brand stories cast the customer or the cause as the hero — and the company as the guide who unlocks the resolution.

The 5-arc framework

Origin. Tension. Decision. Stakes. Resolution.

Five elements. In order. Every brand film, founder series, and category-creation campaign we build is anchored against this arc. The arc is invariant; the content varies wildly.

ARC 1

Origin

The world before the brand. The problem that existed, the people who suffered, the status quo no one questioned. Without origin, the brand has no reason to exist.

ARC 2

Tension

What broke. The friction the founder couldn't ignore. The moment someone in the story decided the status quo was unacceptable. Without tension, there is no story — only an announcement.

ARC 3

Decision

The non-obvious choice. The road not taken. The bet the brand made when an easier path existed. Decision is what separates a brand with conviction from a brand with a category.

ARC 4

Stakes

What happens if the decision is wrong — for the brand, the customer, and the world the brand wants to build. Stakes are the proof that the story is real, not styled.

ARC 5

Resolution

The world after the brand. The customer transformed, the category shifted, the founder vindicated. Resolution doesn't have to be triumphant — it has to be honest.

Map to film

From arc to format.

Each arc has a video format that carries it best. Most brands don't need a single film — they need a system where each format covers one arc.

🎬

Origin → Brand documentary

Long-form, founder-led, archival. The film that earns the audience's trust by showing where the brand came from.

⚡

Tension → Manifesto film

60–90 second cinematic piece. The brand's most provocative claim, delivered with conviction. Built to anchor a campaign.

🛤️

Decision → Founder series

Founder content arc. Why we made the choice, what we turned down, what the bet feels like from inside the company.

🎯

Stakes → Customer story films

Customer-as-hero films. What goes wrong without the brand, what changes with it, what's at risk for the customer.

🏁

Resolution → Category film

The world the brand is building. Less product, more vision. The film that elevates the brand from vendor to category leader.

🔄

System → Always-on retainer

Short-form derivatives, LinkedIn long-form, podcast cuts. The content that keeps the arc alive between anchor films.

FAQ

Questions we get most often.

What's the difference between storytelling and storytelling-washed marketing?

Storytelling has structure — origin, tension, decision, stakes, resolution. Storytelling-washed marketing borrows the vocabulary without the architecture: a few emotive scenes set to a soundtrack, no actual arc. Audiences feel the difference inside fifteen seconds.

How do we use this framework with our existing brand guidelines?

The framework sits underneath the brand guidelines, not on top. Brand guidelines define how the brand looks and sounds; the story framework defines what it actually says. We map them together in the discovery phase.

Internal story vs external story — same or different?

Same arc, different emphasis. The internal story emphasises stakes and decision for the team; the external story emphasises origin and resolution for the audience. Both are built from the same five elements.

How does this work for multi-product companies?

The corporate story sits at the parent level. Each product gets a sub-narrative that ladders up — same five arcs, but tension and stakes localised to the product's audience and competitor set.

How does our team apply this framework?

We run a story diagnostic workshop with founders and brand leads — typically a single day. The output is a written story document, a film-mapping table, and a quarterly content slate. Teams can apply it independently after.
