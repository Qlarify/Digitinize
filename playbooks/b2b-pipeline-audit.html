The B2B Pipeline Audit.

A structured framework for diagnosing why your pipeline isn't growing — and identifying the highest-leverage fix. Takes 2–3 hours with your team. Produces a prioritised action list.

Time to complete: 2–3 hours
Who should run it: Head of Marketing + Head of Sales
What you'll need: CRM access, ad platform data, last 6 months of pipeline data
Output: Prioritised list of pipeline leaks and fixes
Why this matters

Most pipeline problems have the same five root causes.

After ten years of B2B marketing engagements, we see the same pipeline problems repeat across SaaS, IT services, consulting, and fintech. The symptoms vary — low MQL volume, poor MQL-to-SQL conversion, long sales cycles, high CAC — but the root causes are predictable.

🎯

ICP not defined precisely enough

The most common root cause. If your ICP is "mid-market B2B companies," you're not targeting — you're broadcasting. Precise ICP definition (firmographics + technographics + buying triggers) changes every downstream metric.

📉

Channel mix misaligned with deal size

Companies with ₹50L+ ACVs spending the majority of budget on bottom-of-funnel Google Ads. Companies with ₹5L ACVs trying to run enterprise ABM. Channel mix must match deal economics — it almost never does in under-performing programs.

🔧

Broken sales-marketing handoff

Marketing passes leads; sales ignores them. Or sales calls every lead immediately; marketing loses trust in the loop. The handoff definition — what constitutes an MQL, SLA for follow-up, feedback mechanism back to marketing — is where most pipeline leaks.

📊

Attribution not connected to revenue

If your marketing reporting ends at leads or MQLs, you're optimising for a metric that doesn't predict revenue. The audit will identify whether attribution is broken — and what it would take to fix it.

The audit

Five steps. Run in order.

01

Audit your ICP definition

Pull your last 20 closed-won deals. For each one, record: company size, industry vertical, tech stack, buying trigger (what caused them to start evaluating?), and who the economic buyer was. Look for patterns. This is your actual ICP — not the one in the marketing deck.

  • What company size appears most often in closed-won? (revenue range, headcount)
  • Which 2–3 industry verticals make up 70%+ of closed-won revenue?
  • What was the common trigger that started the buying process?
  • What titles are in the buying committee, and who has sign-off authority?
  • Compare this to your current targeting — where is the gap?

Red flag: If your closed-won data shows a clear ICP pattern but your ads, content, and outreach are targeting a broader audience, you're spending 60–80% of your budget on accounts that will never close.

02

Map pipeline drop-off by stage

Pull your CRM data for the last 6 months. Count the number of deals at each stage. Calculate the conversion rate stage-by-stage. Compare to B2B benchmarks.

  • MQL to SQL conversion: industry benchmark is 10–15%. Below 5% = ICP or qualification problem.
  • SQL to opportunity: benchmark 40–60%. Below 30% = messaging or sales motion problem.
  • Opportunity to closed: benchmark 20–30%. Below 15% = competitive positioning or pricing problem.
  • Average sales cycle length vs. 12-month-ago benchmark: is it getting longer?
  • Which stage has the highest drop-off? That's where you start.

What to do with this: Rank your drop-off stages from worst to best. The worst stage is your highest-leverage fix. Everything else is optimisation noise until you fix the primary leak.

03

Audit channel performance against pipeline (not leads)

Pull attribution data for each marketing channel for the last 6 months. For each channel, calculate: spend, MQLs generated, SQLs generated, pipeline sourced, closed-won revenue. If you can't do this because attribution isn't set up — that's your first finding.

  • Which channel has the highest pipeline-per-rupee-spent?
  • Which channel has the lowest cost-per-SQL (not cost-per-lead)?
  • Are there channels generating high lead volume but zero closed-won? Kill them.
  • Is your highest-spend channel also your highest-pipeline channel? If not, why not?
  • What % of closed-won deals had organic search as a first or assisted touch?

Common finding: Most B2B teams discover that one channel is generating 60–70% of pipeline revenue but receiving 20–30% of budget. The audit shows you where to reallocate.

04

Review the sales-marketing handoff

Interview 3 sales reps and your head of sales. Ask: what percentage of marketing-sourced leads do you actually follow up on? Why? What makes a lead worth calling vs. not worth calling? What information do you need from marketing to make a call worthwhile?

  • Is there a written MQL definition that sales and marketing both agree on?
  • What's the SLA for sales follow-up on marketing-sourced leads? Is it being met?
  • Does marketing get feedback on which leads converted and which didn't?
  • Are there re-engagement sequences for leads sales marked "not now"?
  • Can marketing see what happens to a lead after it goes to sales?

The honest question: If sales ignores 50%+ of marketing leads, the problem is usually with the leads — not sales. Fix the qualification logic before blaming the team.

05

Score your findings and prioritise

By now you have a list of problems. Score each one on two axes: pipeline impact (how much pipeline could this unlock?) and effort to fix (weeks, not years). Your priority list is the top-right quadrant: high impact, low effort.

  • List every problem identified in steps 1–4
  • Score each 1–5 for pipeline impact and 1–5 for ease of fix (5 = easiest)
  • Multiply the two scores. Sort descending. Your top 3 are where you start.
  • Anything that scored high impact but high effort: add to 6-month plan
  • Anything that scored low impact: deprioritise entirely — stop debating it

Output: A prioritised action list with owner, timeline, and success metric for each item. This is what you present to the board — not a channel plan, not a content calendar. A pipeline audit output.

The audit checklist

Everything you need to complete the audit.

Pull these data sources before you start. The audit takes 2–3 hours if the data is accessible; 2–3 days if you have to hunt for it.

CRM data

  • Last 20 closed-won deals with firmographic data
  • Pipeline stage conversion rates, last 6 months
  • Average sales cycle by deal size
  • Lead source attribution on closed-won
  • Open pipeline by stage and rep

Marketing platform data

  • Ad spend by channel, last 6 months
  • Leads and MQLs by channel
  • Cost-per-lead by channel
  • Landing page conversion rates
  • Email open and click rates by sequence

SEO and organic data

  • Google Search Console: top queries by clicks
  • Organic traffic trend, last 12 months
  • Top organic landing pages by sessions
  • Organic first-touch on closed-won deals
  • Competitor ranking comparison on category keywords

Sales team inputs

  • Sales rep interview (3 reps, 20 min each)
  • Current MQL definition (written, not assumed)
  • SLA for lead follow-up (written, not assumed)
  • Feedback mechanism from sales to marketing
  • Win/loss analysis from last 10 deals
